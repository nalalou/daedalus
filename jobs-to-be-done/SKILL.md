# Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD) Framework

Customers don't buy products — they "hire" them to make progress in a specific circumstance. A job is the progress a person is trying to make. **It is always stated from the customer's perspective. It is never a business objective.**

---

## When Invoked: Run This Workflow

When a user asks to run a JTBD analysis or identify jobs, follow this process:

### Step 1: Establish the Context

Ask the user:
- What product or decision are we analyzing?
- Who is the customer? (role, situation — not demographics)
- Do you have customer quotes, interview transcripts, or support tickets we can work from?

If they have raw material (quotes, notes), ask them to paste it. Real language is better than assumptions.

### Step 2: Surface the Struggle

Before naming any job, identify the **struggling moment** — the specific circumstance where the customer felt friction, inadequacy, or anxiety. This is the seed of the job.

Ask or infer:
- What was the customer trying to accomplish when they first looked for a solution?
- What were they using before? Why wasn't it good enough?
- What would have happened if they hadn't found a solution?

The struggling moment is the circumstance. Without it, the job is too abstract to be useful.

### Step 3: Write the Job Statement

Use this format:

> **When** [specific circumstance], **I want to** [make this progress], **so I can** [outcome that matters to me].

Good job statements:
- Name a specific circumstance, not a general state
- Describe progress the customer seeks — functional + social + emotional
- Could be said by a real customer about their own life
- Do not mention the product

Bad job statements (reject these):
- Anything that mentions a business metric ("increase retention", "grow revenue")
- Anything the business wants rather than the customer
- Features dressed up as jobs ("easily export to CSV")
- Jobs so broad they're useless ("be more productive")

### Step 4: Identify the Three Dimensions

For each job, map all three dimensions. Missing one is usually why a product loses:

| Dimension | Question to ask | Example |
|-----------|----------------|---------|
| **Functional** | What does the customer literally need to accomplish? | "Get home before my kid's bedtime" |
| **Social** | How do they want to be perceived by others? | "Look like I have my life together" |
| **Emotional** | How do they want to feel? | "Not feel guilty every night" |

The social and emotional dimensions are often where competitors can't follow.

### Step 5: Name the Real Competition

A job's competition is **everything the customer currently hires** to make that progress — not just direct competitors.

Ask: "What did/does the customer do instead?"

Examples:
- Milkshake's morning competition: bananas, granola bars, coffee, nothing
- Condo's competition: staying in the current house, moving to assisted living
- SNHU's competition: not going back to school at all (nonconsumption)

Nonconsumption is often the biggest competitor — and the biggest opportunity.

### Step 6: Test the Job Statement

Apply these checks before calling it done:

**The customer test:** Could a real customer say this about their own life?
- "I need to increase retention" → No. ❌
- "I need to feel confident I made the right call before I sign anything expensive" → Yes. ✅

**The specificity test:** Is the circumstance specific enough to design around?
- "When I'm busy" → Too vague. ❌
- "When I'm context-switching between 10 tasks and losing track of what matters" → Specific enough. ✅

**The product-independence test:** Does the job exist even if the product doesn't?
- If yes → it's a real job ✅
- If no → it's a feature, not a job ❌

### Step 7: Deliver the Output

Present jobs in this format:

```
Job: [job statement in "When / I want to / so I can" format]

Circumstance: [the specific situation that triggers this job]
Functional dimension: [the practical task]
Social dimension: [how they want to be seen]
Emotional dimension: [how they want to feel]

Current competition: [what they hire today, including nonconsumption]
Why current solutions fail: [the specific gap]

Signal this job is real: [workarounds, complaints, or surprising behaviors that point to it]
```

## The Iron Rule

**Jobs are not business objectives.**

| ❌ Not a job | ✅ A job |
|-------------|---------|
| Increase revenue | — |
| Improve retention | — |
| Drive engagement | — |
| Reduce churn | — |
| Grow market share | — |

These are metrics. No customer has ever woken up wanting to help a company reduce churn. If you catch yourself writing one of these, ask: "What is the *customer* trying to accomplish in this moment?"

---

## Common Mistakes to Correct Immediately

**Describing features, not progress**
"The job is to use the dashboard" → Wrong. What decision does the dashboard enable? What anxiety does it remove?

**Using company metrics as jobs**
"The job is to retain users" → Your goal, not their job. Ask what users are trying to accomplish that, if you helped them succeed, would make them stay.

**Making the job too broad**
"The job is to be productive" → Can't design around this. Narrow to a specific circumstance.

**Making the job too narrow**
"The job is to export a CSV" → That's a sub-task. What does the CSV enable?

**Confusing the solution for the job**
"The job is to send an email campaign" → Sending is the solution. The job might be: "Stay relevant to people who haven't bought yet, without annoying them."

## Reference: Key Case Studies

**Morning milkshake** — Commuters hired it to survive a long, boring drive while staying full. Competition: bananas (too fast), donuts (too messy), bagels (too dry). The job existed before milkshakes did.

**SNHU online students** — Working adults hired it to make credential progress without disrupting their job and family. Competition was nonconsumption — most had given up. Redesigning around the job (fast admissions, personal advisers, flexible pacing) produced 34% annual growth.

**American Girl dolls** — Girls hired it to feel connected to history and identity. Parents hired it to share something meaningful with their daughters. Competition: every other toy. None served the emotional and social dimensions. Competitors who focused on the physical product (better dolls, lower prices) captured no market share.

**Condo downsizers** — Retirees hired moving to transition their lives with dignity. The real obstacle: emotional attachment to objects like the dining room table. Adding storage, moving services, and a sorting room solved the emotional job. Prices went up $3,500. Sales grew 25% while the market fell 49%.

The job exists whether or not your product exists. If it doesn't, it's a feature.
