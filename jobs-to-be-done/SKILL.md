# Jobs-To-Be-Done (JTBD) Framework

## Overview

Customers don't buy products — they "hire" them to make progress in a specific circumstance. The **job to be done** is that progress: the functional, social, and emotional outcomes a person is trying to achieve. Demographics describe *who* customers are; jobs describe *why* they buy.

**A job is always stated from the customer's perspective, in the customer's circumstances. It is never a business objective.**

Source: Christensen et al., "Know Your Customers' Jobs to Be Done" (HBR)

---

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- Discovering why customers are churning or not converting
- Defining what problem a product actually solves
- Finding untapped demand ("nonconsumption")
- Designing features, positioning, or messaging
- Evaluating whether a market segment is real
- Explaining why a product succeeded or failed despite strong demographics

Do NOT use for:
- Pure UX/usability research (use task analysis instead)
- A/B testing copy (use behavioral data)
- Feature prioritization without first understanding the job

---

## The Iron Rule: Jobs Are Not Business Objectives

This is the most common failure mode. A job must describe progress the **customer** is trying to make — not what the business wants to achieve.

### Anti-Pattern Examples

| ❌ Business Objective (not a job) | ✅ Actual Job |
|----------------------------------|---------------|
| Increase revenue | — |
| Improve retention | — |
| Drive engagement | — |
| Acquire more users | — |
| Reduce churn | — |
| Grow market share | — |

None of these are jobs. They're metrics. The customer has never once woken up and thought "I need to help this company reduce churn today."

### The Test

Ask: **Could a customer say this about their own life?**

- "I need to increase retention" — No customer says this. ❌
- "I need to get home before my kid's bedtime without being late again" — Yes. ✅
- "I need to feel confident I'm making the right call before I commit to something expensive" — Yes. ✅
- "I need to look like I know what I'm talking about in front of my team" — Yes. ✅

If the answer is no, it's not a job.

## What a Job Actually Looks Like

A job has three dimensions:

| Dimension | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Functional | The practical task | "Get from A to B faster" |
| Social | How others perceive the person | "Look competent in front of my team" |
| Emotional | How the person feels | "Feel in control of a chaotic situation" |

All three matter. A product that nails the functional job but ignores the emotional and social dimensions will lose to one that serves all three.

**Key principle:** Circumstances are more important than customer attributes. The job is defined by the situation, not the person.

---

## How to Write a Job Statement

A well-formed job statement has this structure:

> **When** [circumstance], **I want to** [make this progress], **so I can** [outcome I care about].

Examples:
- "When I'm commuting alone, I want something filling that keeps me occupied without making a mess, so I can arrive focused and not hungry."
- "When I'm downsizing my home, I want to feel like my past matters and will be honored, so I can move on without guilt."
- "When I'm buying something expensive for the first time, I want to feel like I made the smart choice, so I don't feel foolish if it goes wrong."

Notice: none of these mention the product. The job exists whether or not your product exists.

## The Problem with Correlations

Companies segment by demographics (age, income, geography) or product attributes. These correlations are easy to measure but poor predictors of behavior.

> A 44-year-old man buying a truck shares little with another 44-year-old buying a truck — their circumstances and jobs differ entirely.

The fix: **segment by job, not by customer profile.**

## Five Questions for Identifying Jobs

1. **Do you have a job that needs to be done?**
   Look for tasks people are doing imperfectly with existing tools — especially with cobbled-together workarounds.

2. **Where do you see nonconsumption?**
   Who is not using any solution at all, and why? Nonconsumers often have the job but haven't found anything worth hiring.

3. **What work-arounds have people invented?**
   Improvised solutions reveal jobs that no product adequately fills. If people are using spreadsheets, sticky notes, or text messages to do something, there's likely an unmet job.

4. **What tasks do people want to avoid?**
   Negative jobs — things people dread, fear, or find humiliating — are powerful sources of demand. CVS MinuteClinics were built on the job "avoid sitting in the ER for hours for something minor."

5. **What surprising uses have customers invented?**
   Unexpected use cases reveal different, underserved jobs hiding inside your product. NyQuil drinkers using it to sleep revealed the job "fall asleep quickly without feeling sick."

## Case Studies

### The Milkshake Study (Morning vs. Afternoon Job)

A fast food chain studied who was buying milkshakes early in the morning. The buyers were mostly solo commuters — not families. When asked what job they were hiring the milkshake to do:

**Morning job:** "Keep me company and give me something to do during a long, boring commute. I need it to last and fill me up. I'm hiring it against bananas (too fast), donuts (too messy), and bagels (too dry and unsatisfying)."

**Afternoon job (different customer entirely):** Parents buying milkshakes for kids as a treat. Speed matters. Portion size matters. Flavor variety matters.

Same product. Completely different jobs. Separate products would have served each job better. The insight came from the circumstance, not demographics.

### Southern New Hampshire University

Working adults in their 30s had given up on going back to school — not because they didn't want a degree, but because traditional university options were designed for 18-year-olds.

**The job:** "Make progress toward a credential that will move my career forward, on a schedule that doesn't blow up my job and family obligations."

SNHU redesigned everything — admissions speed, advising, course pacing — around that specific job. Competitors weren't other colleges; they were the idea of not going back at all (nonconsumption).

### Intercom

Early customers weren't hiring Intercom to "improve communications." They were hiring it for specific, distinct jobs:

- "Show me who's using my product and what they're actually doing, so I can catch problems before they churn."
- "Let me send the right message to the right user at the right moment — not a blast to everyone."
- "When a customer is stuck, let me help them without making them file a ticket."

Intercom initially sold one bundled product. When they identified these as four distinct jobs, they split into four products — each with a sharper hire reason. Conversion improved because customers could hire just the piece they needed.

### American Girl Dolls

The obvious job framing would be "entertain children." Wrong. That would compete with every toy.

**The actual job:** "Help my daughter feel connected to something bigger than herself — history, identity, her roots — and give us a ritual we can share."

The books, the stores with restaurants, the doll hospital, the historically accurate accessories — all serve the social and emotional dimensions of that job. Disney, Walmart, and Toys "R" Us built better-looking dolls at lower prices and captured essentially no market share. They competed on product attributes. American Girl competed on the job.

### Condo Downsizers

A condo developer targeting retirees couldn't close sales — until they discovered the real job wasn't "buy a smaller home." It was:

**The job:** "Make this terrifying life transition in a way that honors what I'm leaving behind, so I can feel okay about moving on."

The dining room table kept coming up in interviews. Not as a feature request — as a symbol of family history people couldn't bear to discard. The developer added storage, moving services, and a sorting room. They raised prices and grew 25% in a year when the industry fell 49%.

## Quick Reference: Designing Around Jobs

| Design Question | JTBD Answer |
|-----------------|-------------|
| What are we building? | The solution for a specific job in a specific circumstance |
| Who is our customer? | Anyone with this job to do, regardless of demographics |
| What is the competition? | Everything the customer currently hires to do the job (including doing nothing) |
| What does success look like? | The customer makes the progress they were seeking |
| What should we not build? | Features that serve a different job than our core one |

## Common Mistakes

### Describing features, not progress
"The job is to use our dashboard" — No. The job is the progress the customer makes using the dashboard. What decision can they now make? What anxiety goes away?

### Confusing the job with the solution
"The job is to send an email campaign" — No. Sending the email is the solution. The job might be: "Stay top of mind with people who haven't bought yet without annoying them."

### Using company metrics as jobs
"The job is to retain users" — This is your goal, not their job. Ask: what are users trying to accomplish that, if you helped them succeed, would make them stay?

### Making the job too broad
"The job is to be productive" — Too vague to design around. Narrow to a specific circumstance: "When I'm context-switching between 10 open tasks, I want to quickly know what to work on next without having to reconstruct my mental state."

### Making the job too narrow
"The job is to export a CSV" — Too shallow. The CSV is a sub-task. The job is the progress that CSV enables.

---

## Key Takeaway

> The unit of analysis is the job, not the customer, and not the business metric.

When you understand the job customers are trying to do, you can:
- Build offerings that make the hire decision obvious
- Create experiences that span the entire job (before, during, and after)
- Align marketing, product, and process around real demand

Competing on demographics produces undifferentiated products. Competing on business metrics produces feature factories. Competing on jobs produces loyalty.
